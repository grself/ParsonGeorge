Mines

Corner of Fifth and Toughnut Streets

The mines are said to be the 13th most haunted spot in Tombstone.

The mines in Tombstone are known to be haunted and guests who visit them are often rewarded with pictures of orbs and other paranormal activity. Mining is a dangerous occupation and we know of several miners who died violent deaths in Tombstone's early days. The Essential Guide to Boothill Graveyard as given to visitors and Tombstone's Boothill book by Ben Traywick lists these miners:

  • William Alexander: Prospector who unintentionally set off a blast prematurely.
  • Simon Constatine: Killed by a blast.
  • J.D. Dernitt: Fell to his death in a mine shaft.
  • Joseph Eisele: Drilled into a hole that was packed with explosives in the Silver Plume mine.
  • Manuel Elias: Powder man for the Telephone Mine. He set a blast off prematurely and died in the explosion.
  • Thomas Kearney: Killed by a blast.
  • Thomas Martin: Died when the elevator cage in the Silver Thread mine fell on him.
  • J Padden: Fell down the shaft of the Emerald Mine.
  • James Tulley: Jumped from a malfunctioning elevator that was shooting toward the timbers at the top of a shaft. He fell 250 feet to his death.
  • Unknown: Found at the bottom of a 60-foot shaft. He was well dressed so was probably not a miner, but we do not have any clue as to his identity.
  • George Whitcer: Killed when the cable holding his elevator cage broke and it fell to the bottom of the shaft.

It seems reasonable to assume that at least some of these miners, or others who were killed in a mining accident here but are buried elsewhere, are returning today to try to find peace. Guests and tour guides in the mines often report strange noises from back in the mine, along with the occasional unseen touch while on a tour. Photographs of orbs and other paranormal activity are common on the mine tour.

Tour Information: Our trolley passes the entrances to the former Good Enough and Silver Thread mines and I point those locations out to our guests. There were a number of other mine heads along Toughnut streets; however, those were sealed decades ago and we do not stop at any of them. There is a tour into one of the former working mines so guests can access that location and I get a number of photos from down in the mine.
